8:00 PM

It is 8:00 PM, and the sun has set. It is about two hours before you usually fall asleep. You start to wonder about how sleep works.
Here are some facts about the stages of sleep.

  • There are two basic types of sleep: non-rapid eye movement sleep (NREM) and rapid eye movement sleep (REM).
  • Non-rapid eye movement sleep (NREM) has four stages: Stage 1, Stage 2, Stage 3, and Stage 4.
    • Stage 1 is the lightest NREM sleep stage.
    • Stage 2 is characterized by distinct patterns of brain activity: sleep spindles, which are short and synchronous bursts of electrical activity, and K-complexes, which are large spikes of brain wave activity. While sleep spindles consist of multiple brain waves stacked together, K-complexes are usually just a single brain wave.
    • Stages 3 and 4 are deep stages of sleep. Together, stages 3 and 4 are also called slow-wave sleep (SWS), since during Stages 3 and 4, brain waves measured by electroencephalography (EEG) are very slow (slow frequency of about 1-4Hz), large (high amplitude), and synchronous.
  • Rapid eye movement sleep (REM) is a sleep stage characterized by rapid horizontal eye movements. When in REM sleep, your eyes move rapidly back and forth while your eyelids are closed.
    • While dreaming can occur during any stage of sleep, most dreams come from REM sleep.
    • When you go into REM sleep, your brain completely paralyzes all of the voluntary muscles in your body (except your eyes and eye muscles) so that you do not act out your dreams. This is called REM sleep paralysis.
  • In humans, the NREM to REM sleep cycle is approximately 90 minutes. That is, we go down into NREM sleep and then up into REM sleep approximately every 90 minutes. We go through several sleep cycles throughout the night.

12:00 AM

It is 12:00 AM, and you are fast asleep. As you were falling asleep, you reflected on the importance of getting a good night's sleep.
Here are some facts about the benefits of sleep.

  • Sleep improves your memory.
    • Procedural skill memory: Sleep is critical for offline memory consolidation of procedural skill memories, including motor skill memories and visual skill memories. In fact, sleep actually enhances your performance on procedural skill tasks. However, if you do not sleep the first night after learning, you lose the chance for memory consolidation of procedural skill memories and performance improvement on procedural skill tasks.
    • Different stages of sleep provide memory consolidation benefits for different types of memory at different times of the night. For procedural skill memory, motor skill consolidation correlates with Stage 2 NREM sleep in the last quarter of the night, while visual skill consolidation correlates with the combination of slow-wave sleep (SWS) in the first quarter of the night and REM sleep in the last quarter of the night.
    • Declarative memory: Sleep stabilizes declarative fact-based memories and prevents forgetting. Specifically, the quantity and electrical quality of NREM slow-wave sleep (SWS) correlates with declarative memory consolidation.
  • Sleep boosts your immune system.
    • Getting sufficient sleep in the week before getting immunized against the flu helps your immune system produce a strong antibody response to the flu shot. In contrast, getting insufficient sleep (4 hours/night) in the week before getting immunized against the flu results in a weak antibody response to the flu shot that may be less than half of a normal antibody response.
  • Sleep is important for your appearance.
    • In a study, healthy young participants were photographed both after 8 hours of normal sleep and after one night of total sleep deprivation. Independent observers who knew nothing about the study then rated the pictures of the participants based on how tired they looked, how healthy they looked, and how attractive they looked.
    • The study found that participants were rated as looking less tired, more healthy, and more attractive when they had gotten 8 hours of normal sleep (relative to when they had been sleep deprived).

4:00 AM

It is 4:00 AM, and you have awoken from a bad dream. You become curious about why you often have vivid and bizarre dreams.
Here are some facts about dreaming.

  • During REM sleep, regions of the brain involved in emotion, memory, movement initiation, and complex visual processing are activated, while the lateral prefrontal cortex of the brain, which is the part of the brain that controls logical reasoning, is deactivated. This explains why dreams during REM sleep involve emotions, memories, and movement and are visual, bizzare, and illogical.
  • Lucid dreaming is when you are aware that you are dreaming during the act of dreaming itself.
    • Some lucid dreamers can gain control over their dreams and decide what they do in their dreams. For instance, once a lucid dreamer gains control of their dream, they can decide to fly in their dream.
    • While fast electrical brain wave activity is absent over the prefrontal cortex during REM sleep without lucid dreaming, fast electrical brain wave activity is present over the prefrontal cortex during REM sleep with lucid dreaming. That is, during lucid dreaming in REM sleep, the brain wave activity over the prefrontal cortex almost looks like waking activity. This suggests that lucid dreaming happens due to the prefrontal cortex coming back online, which allows you to gain lucid control of your dreaming.
  • REM sleep (dream sleep) is associated with enhancements in creativity, including enhancements in creative problem-solving capacity.

8:00 AM

It is 8:00 AM, and the sun has risen. Last night, you could not fall asleep again after waking up from your bad dream. You begin to feel the effects of sleep deprivation on your body.
Here are some facts about the consequences of sleep deprivation.

  • Short sleep is linked to increased risk for the development of various forms of cancer.
    • Natural killer cells are cells of the immune system that destroy cancerous tumors. A study found that restricting your sleep to 4 hours for one single night reduces your natural killer cell activity by 70%.
    • In 2009, the World Health Organization classified any form of nighttime shift work as a "probable" carcinogen. That is, nighttime shift work may induce cancer due to a disruption of sleep-wake rhythms.
  • Sleep deprivation impacts your attention and concentration.
    • When you are sleep deprived, your brain is having small slices of sleep called microsleeps that you are not aware of. While you think you are staying awake when you are sleep deprived, your brain is actually having microsleeps during which you have lapses in attention.
    • Partial sleep deprivation over time can impair your cognitive performance as much as total sleep deprivation over one night. Specifically, a study found that after 10-12 days of 6 hours of sleep a night, you become as impaired as not sleeping for 24 hours straight (as measured by the number of microsleeps during a sustained attention task). The study also found that after 10-12 days of 4 hours of sleep a night, you become as impaired as not sleeping for 48 hours straight (as measured by the number of microsleeps during a sustained attention task).
    • Drowsy driving is typically more fatal than drunk driving, since when you are driving while drunk, you respond slower to situations, but when you are driving while drowsy and have a microsleep, you do not respond at all.
  • There are many work-related consequences of sleep deprivation, especially in medicine.
    • Medical residents working a 30-hour shift are estimated to make 460% more diagnostic errors in the intensive care unit relative to when they are well-rested. Further, 1 out 5 medical residents working a 30 hour shift are estimated to cause serious harm to a patient due to a fatigue-related error, and 1 out of 20 medical residents working a 30-hour shift are estimated to kill a patient due to a fatigue-related error.
    • Physicians who have not gotten 8 hours of sleep within the last 24 hours are estimated to have a 170% increased risk of causing a major surgical error.
